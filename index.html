Recycling isn’t optional
It’s survival

On Mars, every piece of waste is a chance to reuse and survive. Recycling isn’t optional, it’s essential.

Mars isn’t just a planet of exploration. It’s becoming a planet of waste.

Over 7 tons of debris from robotic missions now lie scattered across its surface. This isn’t just broken metal parts, it includes packaging, textiles, and other materials that reflect our everyday impact.

The REMAV team shows this problem by using real mission data and creative visuals. They take the trash left on Mars and turn it into a story that reminds us of our responsibility as space explorers. As future missions happen especially with humans going to Mars. The amount of waste will grow. Mars is a big place, but it’s still fragile, so every piece of trash counts.

REMAV doesn’t just show the trash. They transform it into treasure. Their goal is to create reusable and sustainable materials from this waste, helping us explore in a way that cares for the planet and the future. This is a reminder to think beyond just discovering Mars. We must explore carefully, because what we leave behind tells the story of humans in space.

Mars Trash Categories

A three year Mars mission could generate over 12,600 kg of inorganic waste, including packaging, textiles, and structural debris, all of which must be reused or repurposed to survive.

Packaging Waste

Boxes, plastic wraps, and containers used to carry supplies. Once opened, they turn into lightweight trash that quickly piles up.

Textile Waste

Clothes, blankets, and fabric wraps used during the mission. After use, they become soft but bulky trash that takes up space.

Structural Waste

Broken parts, damaged panels, and leftover building materials. These are hard and heavy items that are difficult to reuse.
