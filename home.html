Packaging Waste

Food Packaging Trash

During space missions, especially long duration ones like Mars astronauts rely on specially designed packaging to store, protect, and consume their food. These packages are engineered to be lightweight, durable, and safe for use in microgravity or low gravity. Over time, as meals are consumed, these wrappers and containers become non-edible waste, forming a major part of the trash generated onboard.

Unlike Earth, where trash can be collected and processed externally, astronauts must manage waste on-site. That’s why understanding food packaging trash is essential not just for hygiene and space management, but for sustainability and recycling.

Multi-layer meal pouches

Used for storing main meals like pasta or scrambled eggs. Made from laminated layers of foil and plastic to block moisture and oxygen. After use, they become bulky waste due to their rigid structure and layered composition.

Plastic Drink Bags

Flexible pouches used for water and rehydrated beverages. Designed with straws and clamps to prevent spills in microgravity. Once emptied, they contribute soft plastic waste that accumulates daily.

Condiment Tubes & Sachets

Single use packets for ketchup, mustard, salt, and sugar. Small in size but high in volume over time. Their composite materials make them harder to compact, adding to the complexity of waste management.

Plastic liners

Plastic liners are thin, flexible sheets used to wrap and protect cargo during transit. Once unpacked, they become lightweight but persistent waste inside the Mars habitat.

Bubble wrap & foam inserts

Bubble wrap and foam inserts protect equipment by absorbing shocks during launch and landing. Once unpacked, they become lightweight but bulky waste that adds to the habitat’s trash.

Cardboard boxes

Cardboard boxes store and protect supplies on Mars missions. Once unpacked, they become bulky waste that astronauts must manage carefully.

Cargo & Equipment Packaging Trash

During a Mars mission, astronauts receive tools, instruments, and supplies packed in protective materials designed to withstand the stress of launch, landing, and surface operations. These include plastic liners that wrap cargo, foam inserts and bubble wrap that cushion delicate equipment, cardboard boxes for organizing dry goods and medical kits, and thermal blankets that shield gear from extreme temperatures. Each material serves a critical role in ensuring the safe delivery of mission essentials.

Once the cargo is unpacked and deployed, these packaging materials lose their functional value and become part of the habitat’s waste stream. Unlike Earth, where trash can be discarded or recycled externally, Mars missions must manage all waste locally. This makes cargo packaging not just leftover debris, but a potential resource — one that can be repurposed into insulation, structural panels, or storage solutions to support long-term survival and sustainability.

Medical & Hygiene Packaging Trash

Medical and hygiene packaging trash includes the wrappers, tubes, and packets used to keep personal care and medical items sterile and accessible during space missions. Astronauts rely on these materials daily — from wipe packets and toothpaste tubes to sterile wrappers for medical tools. Though small in size, they accumulate quickly and become part of the habitat’s waste stream. On Mars, where external disposal isn’t possible, managing this trash is essential for maintaining cleanliness, safety, and long-term sustainability.

Sterile wrappers

Sterile wrappers protect medical tools and wipes by keeping them clean and contamination-free. After use, they become lightweight waste that must be managed carefully during Mars missions.

Toothpaste tubes & wipe packets

Toothpaste tubes and wipe packets are used for daily hygiene on Mars missions. Once emptied, they become small but frequent waste items that add to the habitat’s trash load.
