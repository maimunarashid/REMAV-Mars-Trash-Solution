Mars

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun, and the seventh largest. It’s the only planet we know of inhabited entirely by robots.

Mars Trash Categories

A three year Mars mission could generate over 12,600 kg of inorganic waste, including packaging, textiles, and structural debris, all of which must be reused or repurposed to survive.

Packaging Waste

Boxes, plastic wraps, and containers used to carry supplies. Once opened, they turn into lightweight trash that quickly piles up.

Textile Waste

Clothes, blankets, and fabric wraps used during the mission. After use, they become soft but bulky trash that takes up space.

Structural Waste

Broken parts, damaged panels, and leftover building materials. These are hard and heavy items that are difficult to reuse.

Packaging Waste

Food Packaging Trash

During space missions, especially long duration ones like Mars astronauts rely on specially designed packaging to store, protect, and consume their food. These packages are engineered to be lightweight, durable, and safe for use in microgravity or low gravity. Over time, as meals are consumed, these wrappers and containers become non-edible waste, forming a major part of the trash generated onboard.

Unlike Earth, where trash can be collected and processed externally, astronauts must manage waste on-site. That’s why understanding food packaging trash is essential not just for hygiene and space management, but for sustainability and recycling.

Multi-layer meal pouches

Used for storing main meals like pasta or scrambled eggs. Made from laminated layers of foil and plastic to block moisture and oxygen. After use, they become bulky waste due to their rigid structure and layered composition.

Plastic Drink Bags

Flexible pouches used for water and rehydrated beverages. Designed with straws and clamps to prevent spills in microgravity. Once emptied, they contribute soft plastic waste that accumulates daily.

Condiment Tubes & Sachets

Single use packets for ketchup, mustard, salt, and sugar. Small in size but high in volume over time. Their composite materials make them harder to compact, adding to the complexity of waste management.

Plastic liners

Plastic liners are thin, flexible sheets used to wrap and protect cargo during transit. Once unpacked, they become lightweight but persistent waste inside the Mars habitat.

Bubble wrap & foam inserts

Bubble wrap and foam inserts protect equipment by absorbing shocks during launch and landing. Once unpacked, they become lightweight but bulky waste that adds to the habitat’s trash.

Cardboard boxes

Cardboard boxes store and protect supplies on Mars missions. Once unpacked, they become bulky waste that astronauts must manage carefully.

Cargo & Equipment Packaging Trash

During a Mars mission, astronauts receive tools, instruments, and supplies packed in protective materials designed to withstand the stress of launch, landing, and surface operations. These include plastic liners that wrap cargo, foam inserts and bubble wrap that cushion delicate equipment, cardboard boxes for organizing dry goods and medical kits, and thermal blankets that shield gear from extreme temperatures. Each material serves a critical role in ensuring the safe delivery of mission essentials.

Once the cargo is unpacked and deployed, these packaging materials lose their functional value and become part of the habitat’s waste stream. Unlike Earth, where trash can be discarded or recycled externally, Mars missions must manage all waste locally. This makes cargo packaging not just leftover debris, but a potential resource — one that can be repurposed into insulation, structural panels, or storage solutions to support long-term survival and sustainability.

Medical & Hygiene Packaging Trash

Medical and hygiene packaging trash includes the wrappers, tubes, and packets used to keep personal care and medical items sterile and accessible during space missions. Astronauts rely on these materials daily — from wipe packets and toothpaste tubes to sterile wrappers for medical tools. Though small in size, they accumulate quickly and become part of the habitat’s waste stream. On Mars, where external disposal isn’t possible, managing this trash is essential for maintaining cleanliness, safety, and long-term sustainability.

Sterile wrappers

Sterile wrappers protect medical tools and wipes by keeping them clean and contamination-free. After use, they become lightweight waste that must be managed carefully during Mars missions.

Toothpaste tubes & wipe packets

Toothpaste tubes and wipe packets are used for daily hygiene on Mars missions. Once emptied, they become small but frequent waste items that add to the habitat’s trash load.

Textile Waste

Clothing

During a Mars mission, astronauts rely on specialized clothing like flight suits, thermal layers, socks, and gloves for protection, temperature control, and hygiene. Over time, these garments wear out, become damaged, or lose effectiveness — turning into bulky textile waste.

Instead of discarding used clothing, crews must find ways to repurpose it into useful materials like insulation, cleaning cloths, or patch kits to reduce reliance on Earth-supplied resources.

Flight suits

Worn during launch and landing for safety. They wear out and become trash over time.

Gloves & socks

Protect hands and feet during daily tasks. They get dirty or torn and turn into waste.

Blankets & wraps

Used to keep astronauts warm and protect gear. Over time, they tear or wear out and become trash.

Thermal covers

Used to shield equipment from heat and cold. Once damaged or replaced, they turn into waste.

Insulation Fabrics

Insulation fabrics are used to protect astronauts and equipment from extreme Mars temperatures and dust. They line sleeping areas, wrap sensitive gear, and cover habitat walls. Over time, these materials wear out, tear, or get replaced — turning into bulky textile waste.

Instead of discarding them, crews must repurpose insulation fabrics into thermal padding, equipment wraps, or wall reinforcements to reduce waste and extend mission resources.

Straps & Belts

Straps and belts are used throughout a Mars mission to secure equipment, fasten gear, and stabilize supplies in low gravity. They’re essential for safety and organization inside the habitat and during transport. Over time, these items can fray, lose strength, or break — turning into durable textile waste.

Instead of discarding them, crews must find ways to reuse straps and belts as tie-downs, tension supports, or DIY harnesses to reduce waste and extend mission resources.

Gear harnesses

Gear harnesses hold tools and supplies so they don’t float away in space. Over time, they get worn out and become trash. Find ways to use old harnesses again to save materials and reduce waste.

Tie-downs

Used to keep equipment and supplies secure in low gravity. When worn out, they can be reused to help hold things in place and reduce waste.

Structural Waste

Protective Structures

These parts shield the spacecraft and habitat from heat, dust, and impact. Items like heat shields, dust covers, and impact panels are critical during landing and setup. But once they’re burned, torn, or no longer needed, they become bulky trash that must be reused or repurposed.

Heat Shield

Heat Shields protect the spacecraft from extreme heat during entry into the Martian atmosphere. They absorb intense friction and often burn or crack, making them unusable after landing. Because they cannot be repaired or reused, heat shields become heavy, damaged waste left on the surface.

Dust Covers

Dust Covers shield equipment from the fine and pervasive Martian dust that can clog and damage sensitive instruments. Over time, these covers tear or wear out due to harsh conditions, losing their protective ability and becoming disposable waste.

Aluminum Frames

Aluminum Frames provide lightweight, strong support for shelves, gear racks, and sections of the habitat. These frames can become bent, broken, or obsolete when habitats are reconfigured or upgraded, resulting in structural waste that must be managed.

Mounting Brackets

Mounting Brackets are used to attach tools, devices, and equipment securely to walls or structures. They can become damaged, stripped, or unnecessary as the habitat layout changes, turning them into discarded materials that accumulate over time.

Frames & Supports

Frames and supports hold up shelves, gear racks, and sections of the habitat. They help keep things stable in low gravity. Over time, they may bend, break, or become unnecessary after layout changes — turning into rigid waste that can be reused for building or repairs.

Tubes & Rods

Tubes and rods carry air, wires, or help support structures. Plastic tubes may crack or clog, and metal rods can bend or get replaced during upgrades. These leftover parts add to structural waste but can be reshaped or reused for tools and frames.

Plastic Tubes

Plastic Tubes serve to carry air, fluids, or wiring safely throughout the habitat. They may crack, clog, or degrade during use, and when replaced or removed during system upgrades, they add to the volume of plastic waste.

Metal Rods

Metal Rods are used to support equipment, act as handles, or form part of structural frameworks. These rods can become bent, broken, or redundant during repairs and upgrades, contributing to the pile of structural waste that must be creatively reused or recycled.
